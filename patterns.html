GREY is the meeting point of RGB (light) and CMY (pigment). Six typographic treatments, each encoding the six channels with restraint and intent — not decoration. Every specimen is presented on dark and on paper, calibrated for apparel print at 1:1 and for screen at any scale.

The document

A type specimen in the tradition of Monotype foundries — the word set in a refined editorial serif, its six channels annotated as laboratory data. Authority comes from restraint; the colour lives in the metadata, not the letterform.

The press error

An offset print where the C, M and Y plates drifted half a millimeter. What reads as a printing mistake is, in this case, deliberate: the failure of the plates to align is the proof that GREY exists as the sum of its channels. Quiet, analogue, nearly historical.

The equation

A statement. The philosophy rendered as a brief mathematical proof: light adds to grey; pigment adds to grey; observation passes through. The word sits in italic serif, immovable. Below it, the mono channels line up like code comments — footnotes to the display line.

The issued item

Constructed like an archival record or admission tag. A tiny header, the word as a catalogue name, and the six channels compressed into a bar that reads as both a barcode and a visible spectrum. The form is bureaucratic; the content is the brand's entire philosophy.

The absence

Six coloured stripes — R G B C M Y — cross the frame unbroken. The word GREY is not rendered; it is cut away from them, a silhouette through which the page shows through. Presence is the colour; the word is the seeing.

The seven observations

Seven renderings of the same word, each in a different weight and channel: from airy red light through the dense centre (the display, grey, bold) and out to the yellow shadow of pigment. A type specimen for the idea that how you observe a thing is what the thing becomes.
